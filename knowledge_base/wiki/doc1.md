# Climate Change: Causes and Impacts

## What is Climate Change?

Climate change refers to long-term shifts in global temperatures and weather patterns, primarily driven by human activities. The most significant driver is the increase in atmospheric carbon dioxide (CO2) and other greenhouse gases released through fossil fuel combustion, deforestation, and industrial processes. Since the mid-20th century, the warming of the atmosphere has been unequivocal, with most of this warming occurring in the past 40 years.

The Earth's climate system is incredibly complex, involving interactions between the atmosphere, oceans, ice sheets, and living organisms. Small changes in solar radiation, volcanic activity, and Earth's orbital parameters can influence climate over geological timescales, but the current warming trend is primarily attributed to human influence.

## Causes of Global Warming

The primary cause of modern climate change is the greenhouse effect intensified by human activities. Greenhouse gases, including carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide, and fluorinated gases, trap heat in the atmosphere by absorbing infrared radiation that would otherwise escape to space.

Key sources of greenhouse gas emissions include:
- **Fossil fuel combustion**: Burning coal, oil, and natural gas for energy accounts for about 75% of global greenhouse gas emissions
- **Agriculture**: Livestock farming produces methane, and fertilizers release nitrous oxide
- **Deforestation**: Removing forests reduces the planet's capacity to absorb CO2
- **Industrial processes**: Manufacturing and chemical production release various greenhouse gases
- **Waste management**: Landfills produce methane as organic matter decomposes

The concentration of CO2 in the atmosphere has increased from about 280 ppm before industrialization to over 410 ppm today, representing a 46% increase.

## Impacts and Consequences

Climate change is producing observable effects across the globe. Average global temperatures have risen approximately 1.1°C above pre-industrial levels, and this warming is not uniform - polar regions are warming faster than the tropics (a phenomenon known as Arctic amplification).

Major impacts include:
- **Sea level rise**: Thermal expansion of ocean water and melting of glaciers and ice sheets are raising sea levels, threatening coastal communities
- **Extreme weather events**: Increasing frequency and intensity of hurricanes, droughts, floods, and heatwaves
- **Ecosystem disruption**: Species migration, altered breeding patterns, and potential extinction of vulnerable species
- **Agricultural challenges**: Changes in growing seasons and water availability affecting crop production
- **Human health risks**: Heat-related illnesses, expanded range of vector-borne diseases, and mental health impacts from ecological anxiety

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) projects that without significant emissions reductions, global temperatures could rise 2.7°C by 2100, leading to catastrophic consequences for human civilization.
